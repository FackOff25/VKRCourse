Graph-structured data have become a fundamental component of modern information systems, including social networks, recommendation engines, knowledge graphs, transportation networks, communication infrastructures etc. As the size of these graphs continues to grow, graph databases increasingly rely on distributed architectures, where graph data are partitioned across multiple storage nodes. While distribution enables horizontal scalability, it also introduces the challenge of efficiently assigning graph vertices to partitions.

The quality of graph partitioning has a direct impact on the performance of distributed graph databases. During graph traversals and shortest-path queries, communication between storage nodes is required whenever adjacent vertices reside in different partitions. Such inter-partition communication incurs significantly higher latency than local memory access and can potentially become the dominant factor limiting system performance. Consequently, an effective partitioning strategy should simultaneously minimize the number of cut edges while maintaining a balanced distribution of data across storage nodes.

Numerous graph partitioning techniques have been proposed to address this problem. Some databases chose to adress partitioning quality issue and focus on balancing, like ArangoDB \cite{arangodb_github}. Traditional solutions, such as METIS \cite{MetisOG}, produce high-quality partitions by exploiting the structural properties of the graph. However, these methods are primarily designed for static graphs and often require global repartitioning, making them computationally expensive for large-scale or dynamically evolving datasets. In contrast, streaming partitioning algorithms, such as Fennel \cite{Fennel}, provide scalable and efficient placement of vertices as they arrive, balancing partition sizes while reducing potential edge cuts. Despite their computational efficiency, streaming methods are inherently greedy and may produce suboptimal partitions that cannot be improved without additional refinement. They also lack the awareness of whole graph they partition which might lead to very suboptimal partitioning with bad order of vertices addition.

This work proposes a hybrid graph partitioning approach that combines the scalability of the Fennel streaming algorithm with the local optimization capabilities of the Kernighan–Lin algorithm. In the proposed method, Fennel is employed to construct an initial balanced partitioning of the graph, after which KL refinement is applied to reduce the number of inter-partition edges while preserving partition balance. The combination aims to achieve a favorable trade-off between partitioning efficiency and partition quality, making it suitable for large distributed graph databases where both scalability and communication cost are critical considerations.

Graph models are exceptionally well-suited for representing systems and phenomena characterized by diverse internal connections. They allow for storing and analyzing not only the attribute information of entities (vertices) but also contextual information based on edges. This is particularly valuable for developing computational technology applications in fields such as artificial intelligence, biology, medicine, sociology, microcircuit design, and many others \cite{MajeedRaufGraphTheory2020}. However, the relevance and attention devoted to graph research and analysis algorithms have not yet led to significant changes in the architecture of computing systems. Processing graph structures remains one of the most computationally intensive tasks, facing fundamental limitations of classical von Neumann-type architectures. Traditional processors demonstrate high performance with regular data structures, but their efficiency drastically drops when processing irregular structures, such as semantic networks, social graphs, and biological systems.

The core problem lies in the semantic gap between the nature of graph data and the architecture of computing systems. General-purpose processors operate on bits, bytes, and memory addresses, whereas graphs require operating with entities, relationships, and their semantics. This gap forces the system to perform costly transformations: high-level graph operations must be decomposed into millions of low-level processor instructions, leading to significant overhead.

A specific example of this architectural inadequacy is the "memory wall" \cite{WangAcomprehensive2016}, exacerbated by the inherently irregular nature of data access. Modern microprocessors employ multi-level caches \cite{XuGraphprocessingonGPU2014}, which yield maximum benefit when working with regular data structures. However, graph algorithms generate chaotic memory access patterns, where traversing from one vertex to another means jumping to a random memory location. This results in a high cache miss rate—for graph algorithms, this rate can reach up to 95\% (with an average of around 60\% for the set of commonly used algorithms such as Page rank or SSSP), compared to an average of about 10\% for computational algorithms \cite{XuGraphprocessingonGPU2014}.

Another characteristic feature of graph processing algorithms is the significant dominance of data access time over computation time \cite{popovImplementationFordFulkersonAlgorithm2014,popovStudyKruskalPrim2014,IbrahimAnalysisx862010}. The latency of accessing data not present in cache memory can exceed 1000 clock cycles \cite{QianWeiXiangXiaoAPerformance2021}, meaning that only about 10\% of the execution time of graph algorithms is spent on direct arithmetic and logical processing.

An architectural paradox also manifests in the mismatch between the principles of input/output organization in modern computing systems and the requirements of graph processing. Bus interfaces and microprocessor pipelines are optimized for batch and large-block data transfer \cite{QianWeiXiangXiaoAPerformance2021}, while working with sets of vertices and edges requires individual access to elements \cite{PIUMA2020}, with small data block sizes typically ranging from 4 to 16 bytes.

The problem is further aggravated by physical limitations on memory bandwidth. In high-performance CPUs, the number of memory channels (typically 2–12) is significantly lower than the number of execution threads. For instance, in Intel Xeon Max series 6500/6700 processors with 12 memory channels, the number of threads can reach 172. Our tests show that a single thread performing intensive memory access is sufficient to saturate the bandwidth of one DDR4 or DDR5 memory channel. Even the use of HBM (High Bandwidth Memory) does not fully resolve the issue. Research \cite{McCaplinBandwithLimits2023} indicates the maximum achieved performance is only about 43\% of the peak theoretical bandwidth.

Graphics Processing Units (GPUs) demonstrate higher memory bandwidth and can be efficient for tasks with regular graph structures \cite{AasawatHowWell2018}. However, with irregular graph structures, memory access patterns become random and unique to each thread, leading to severe performance degradation. Short algorithm cycles and frequent branching prevent effective utilization of the SIMT (Single Instruction, Multiple Threads) architecture \cite{GridGAS2018}. Moreover, HBM memory remains a costly solution, and further increases in its bandwidth face serious physical and economic constraints.


The issue of energy efficiency is also becoming critical. When executing graph operations on general-purpose processors, a large portion of the hardware remains underutilized \cite{XuGraphprocessingonGPU2014}. Given that cache memory can occupy up to 40\% of a chip's die area, the high cache miss rate in graph algorithms significantly reduces the metric of million traversed edges per second per watt (MTEPS/W) \cite{GreenGraph500}. Transporting data through multi-level cache hierarchies and core idling while waiting for data degrade the computations-per-watt metric \cite{zhouHighThroughputEnergyEfficientGraph2016}.

Analysis of specialized systems reveals alternative architectural approaches. The Green Graph 500 ranking demonstrates diverse strategies: specialized architectures like Tianhe-HNU and RAZIEL achieve high energy efficiency (over 22,000 GTEPS/W) through custom hardware optimizations, while research systems like GraphCREST demonstrate that FPGA-based solutions can achieve 50-250 GTEPS/W at low power budgets (2-40 W). In contrast, conventional server architectures optimized for general-purpose workloads, such as the Todi system (79 kW, 0.2 GTEPS/W), prioritize absolute performance over energy efficiency for graph processing.

This dichotomy illustrates a fundamental design trade-off: systems optimized for peak graph processing efficiency typically employ specialized architectures with application-specific optimizations, whereas general-purpose systems maintain flexibility across diverse workloads at the cost of graph-specific efficiency. The variations in energy efficiency metrics across different system classes reflect underlying architectural choices rather than absolute superiority of any single approach.

Thus, the further processing of knowledge graphs cannot be solved efficiently (in terms of time, energy cost, and financial cost) within existing paradigms (Fig.~\ref{fig:intro} a,b). Investigation of various approaches has shown the relevance of developing specialized hardware and software. Among the promising directions are: moving processing units into memory \cite{AhnAScalable2015,GraphDEAGraphProcessing2019}; improving the efficiency of core communication for multiple memory accesses \cite{PIUMA2020}; implementing parallel computing structures \cite{PIUMA2020,HighlyScalable2016}; and creating multithreaded cores coupled with an increased number of independent memory channels \cite{ASurveyonGraph2019,DomainSpecificArch2012,popovTeragraphHeterogeneousSystem2022} (Fig.~\ref{fig:intro} c).

The cumulative evidence demonstrates that efficient large-scale graph processing cannot be achieved through evolutionary improvements to existing architectures. Instead, it demands a deep rethinking of computing system design that addresses the semantic gap between graph operations and hardware execution. This paper responds to this challenge by presenting the Teragraph heterogeneous computing system, whose architecture is specifically designed around the principles of graph processing: associative memory access, parallel execution on irregular structures, and hierarchical organization that respects the unique characteristics of graph data and algorithms.

This research presents a refined concept of a computing system \cite{popovTeragraphHeterogeneousSystem2022,popovIntroductionMISDTechnology2017} for operating with large-scale graphs and reports the results of analytical and design work conducted.
